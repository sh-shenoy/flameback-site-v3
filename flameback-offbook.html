Off-book

Things we’ve built that have nothing to do with money.

Three apps so far — a pet-adoption marketplace, a missing-pet network, and an open-source wellness tracker built for Indian food and medicine. None are commercial. None are loss leaders. We build them because someone on the team saw a problem worth solving and had the means to solve it.

The firm’s view, in one paragraph.

A serious firm is allowed to care about things besides its bottom line. We can build software, and some problems close to our daily lives don’t have good software yet. So we build it, ship it, and walk away.

None of these exist to generate leads, build a brand, or earn anything. They’re side projects from a team that takes its work seriously — and its time off seriously too. If they help someone, that’s the whole point.

project i

OnlyAdopt.

A place to adopt pets, not buy them. Dogs, cats, rabbits, birds, and the occasional turtle.

Live · onlyadopt.in

India has tens of thousands of animals in shelters looking for homes — and a parallel business selling animals for profit. OnlyAdopt tries to make adoption the easy path: clean listings, verified shelters, photo-first browsing, location filters, and a clear route from interest to home visit.

No fees. No paid promotion. Shelters list free, adopters browse free, and we pay the hosting bill.

project ii

findingMarx.

A missing-pet network — lost-and-found infrastructure for India’s city pet families.

Live · in production · second deployment

It started as Finding Marx, a real emergency for a real dog, and survives as a reusable system. A pet goes missing; an alert page is live in under two minutes; word spreads across neighbourhood networks by WhatsApp, posters, and geo-tagged sightings. The same engine has since been used for Finding Ollie.

The design is deliberately simple: web-first (works on any phone, no download), location-aware, reward-trackable, and free for the family searching. Sightings are crowd-sourced and triangulated, with the closest matches pushed first.

project iii

Vitality.

An open-source wellness tracker built for Indian food and medicine.

Live · open source · MIT

Every tracker on the App Store assumes you eat Western food, take American-brand medicine, and live in a Western climate. We built Vitality for ourselves and then opened it up — an Indian-first health tracker with real food entries (dosa, sabzi, dal, paratha), a medication database keyed to Indian brand names, a body map for symptoms, and an AI coach that won’t suggest cottage cheese.

The code is on GitHub. The hosted version is on Railway, free, no account needed for basic tracking.
