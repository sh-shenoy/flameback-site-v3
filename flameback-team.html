i. PRINCIPLE
Engineering-first
In practice

Everyone on the investment side writes code.

a.The rule

We have one structural rule that decides almost everything else: everyone with authority over investment decisions also has commits in the production codebase. No gap between the people who think about markets and the people who build what the thinking produces. Every model that allocates client capital was written, debugged, paper-traded, and deployed by someone who can explain not just what it does but why every line does it that way.

This sounds obvious. In practice, almost no asset manager works this way. The norm is that portfolio managers describe their intent in PowerPoint and analyst notes, and engineering teams build — with a translation layer in between that loses fidelity at every step. In 2017 we removed the translation layer.

A strategy is a piece of working code. If you cannot write it, you do not understand it.
b.What this changes

You can see it in how we work day to day. Strategies are version-controlled, not documented. When the investment committee changes a position-sizing rule, the change is a pull request, reviewed line by line, with a test suite that has to pass before merge. There is no “the model said X but we’re going to do Y because we have a feel for it.” Overrides happen as code, with an auditable history.

This also changes who we hire. Most candidates fail one of two tests — they can do the finance but not the engineering, or the engineering but not the finance. The overlap is small. We interview slowly, often for several months, before making an offer.

iii. STRUCTURE
Fifteen people
Four pillars

How the team is organised.

Beyond the named leadership, the firm runs on four functional pillars. Most people work across more than one at a time — the pillars describe the work, not the silos.

i. Research & engineering ~ 7 people

The largest pillar. Builds the strategies, the back-test infrastructure, the FIST engine, the data pipelines, the QX research environment. Everyone here writes code daily and is expected to hold a working view on at least one of the firm’s active research questions.

ii. Operations & risk ~ 3 people

Daily reconciliation, NAV calculation, trade settlement, custodian reconciliation, risk dashboards. The work that’s invisible when right and catastrophic when wrong. Standards here are unforgiving by design.

iii. Client service ~ 2 people

Relationships with existing clients, onboarding for new ones, quarterly reviews, ad-hoc questions. Each relationship manager carries a small book by design — depth, not breadth.

iv. Compliance & legal ~ 2 people + external

SEBI and SEC filings, audit support, legal documentation, regulatory monitoring. Backed by external counsel for material matters. Files weeks before deadline, as a matter of practice.

b.What we test for

Most candidates fail one of two specific tests. The first is engineering — can they write production code under time pressure, reason about systems that have to run for years untouched, read someone else’s code and improve it. The second is financial literacy — do they understand what a Sharpe ratio actually measures, why volatility is not risk, what a regime is, what makes a strategy decay.

A few pass both. The last test is softer: do they have the temperament for an infinite game? Will they still be doing this in five years, in ten, when the markets have changed, the strategies have evolved, the clients have grown, and the firm is recognisably the same place? We hire people who plan to stay.

v. VALUES
What the team
Believes in practice

Values, in working order.

Most firms publish values. Few describe how the team actually behaves. Here are four we see in our work daily — not aspirations, but observations.

i.

Quiet over loud.

We prefer a small careful claim to a large impressive one. Nobody here is comfortable being quoted on business television. Research notes are written as if the reader is a sceptical colleague, not a prospect.

In practice: no public predictions
ii.

Writing over meetings.

Most decisions happen in writing first. A proposal becomes a memo, the memo is reviewed asynchronously, the meeting (if any) is for the dissent. Most meetings we do have run shorter than thirty minutes.

In practice: written decision logs
iii.

Patience as operating system.

Strategies live for years. Relationships compound over decades. We refuse to rush research, to onboard clients faster than operations can support, to grow the team faster than the existing team can absorb. Patience is not a virtue here; it is the architecture.

In practice: slow hiring, slow scaling
iv.

Candour, especially uncomfortable.

When a strategy underperforms, the client letter says so, in the first paragraph, with the reason. When a position-sizing rule changes, the change is documented and dated. When we get something wrong, the post-mortem is written, shared internally, and referenced in future decisions. The firm is built on the assumption that honesty compounds.

In practice: open post-mortems
vi. ALIGNMENT
The principal
The team

What it means that the founders’ capital runs alongside.

a.The structural point

Every member of the leadership team has a material portion of their personal capital invested in the firm’s strategies. Not symbolically. Not a token allocation to signal alignment. Substantially. When the strategies have a hard quarter, the principals lose money personally, in the same proportion, with no separate hedge.

This is the only credible form of alignment in asset management. Fee structures can be made to look aligned without being so. Performance fees with the wrong hurdle, high-water marks that reset on subscription, share classes that spread risk unevenly — the industry is full of arrangements that look fair on paper and aren’t. The only test that matters is whether the person making the decisions feels the same loss the client feels.

We pass that test. It’s also why we hire slowly — over time, new senior members will put their own capital alongside the principals’, and the alignment broadens naturally. It is something the team grows into, not a marketing claim.
