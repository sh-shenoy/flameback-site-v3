i. THE FRAME
Why this firm
writes its own software

Most investment firms buy their stack. This one didn’t.

a.The decision in 2017

When the firm was founded in 2017, the standard practice was to buy. Order management from one vendor, portfolio accounting from another, a research toolkit from a third, a back-test framework from a fourth. The components rarely spoke to each other. The cost of stitching them together — in engineering time, in reconciliation overhead, in latency, in opacity — was paid forever.

The founders made a different decision. We would write our own for the parts that determine edge: the trade engine, the research environment, the data layer, and eventually the agent layer that sits on top of them. The parts that do not determine edge — the broker, the custodian, the exchange, the cloud — we would buy, like everyone else.

That decision compounds. Eight years later, we run on software we understand at every line. If something breaks, the person who can fix it works in the same office. If a research hypothesis needs a new data field, we add it to the data lake the same week. If an execution algorithm needs to change, the engineer changes it.

a.How to read the diagram

The data lake sits at the bottom. Everything we trade on, monitor, or back-test is in it — tick data, fundamentals, filings, news, corporate actions. Two systems read from it: FIST, which trades, and QX, which researches. Above them sits Jarvis, the agent layer — the LLM-backed workflows the team uses to run the firm without drowning in keystrokes.

The bought components — brokers, exchanges, custodians, cloud — sit at the perimeter. They have replacements. The four written components in the middle do not.

Written: Jarvis · FIST · QX · Data lake  ·  Bought: cloud, brokers, exchanges, custodians, KYC

i.
TRADE ENGINE · LIVE SINCE OCTOBER 2017

FIST — the trade engine.

~8 yrslive production
0missed open / close

FIST — the firm’s order management and execution kernel — is the oldest piece of software at the firm. It went live on a single strategy in October 2017 and has not had an unplanned outage during market hours since. It is the part of the stack that touches client capital.

What it does, end to end: receives signals from the research environment, applies pre-trade risk gates (concentration, leverage, sector caps, drawdown limits), routes orders to brokers via FIX, manages execution algorithms (VWAP, TWAP, implementation-shortfall variants), reconciles fills against intended positions in real time, and writes every action to an audit log that is unalterable.

It is deliberately conservative. It does not get rewritten because someone read a new paper. Changes go through code review, paper-trading validation, and a staged rollout. The cost of bugs is paid in client capital, not in engineering time.

LANGUAGECore in Python with C++ extensions for the order book and risk-gate hot path. FIX 4.4 to brokers.
UPTIMETwo redundant production instances. Failover under thirty seconds. No missed market opens or closes since launch.
DEPLOY CADENCEWeekly small-change windows on Saturdays. Major releases only outside trading hours and only after paper-trade validation.
AUDITEvery order, fill, and risk-gate decision logged in an append-only store. Used in monthly reconciliation, quarterly compliance reviews, annual audit.
ii.
DATA LAKE · THE SUBSTRATE

The data lake — everything the firm trades on, in one place.

~14 TBtick & reference
2018tick-level since

Quantitative work is bottlenecked by data quality long before it is bottlenecked by ideas. The firm’s data lake is the answer to that bottleneck. It holds tick-level price data for the Indian universe since 2018, point-in-time fundamentals back further, corporate actions, filings, regulatory disclosures, and news. Everything is survivorship-clean. Everything is point-in-time.

The discipline of point-in-time matters more than the size. A back-test that uses today’s knowledge of which companies still exist is a back-test that lies. The lake is structured so that every query, on every date, returns only what was knowable on that date.

Ingestion runs daily after market close. Reconciliation against multiple feeds catches discrepancies before they reach research. The firm has rejected back-tests in the past because a single field couldn’t be sourced cleanly. The data is the substrate. Compromising on the substrate compromises everything above it.

STORAGEPostgres for reference and corporate-action data. ClickHouse for tick and high-cardinality time-series.
FEEDSMultiple primary providers per asset class. Cross-checked daily. Discrepancies investigated before research consumers read.
COVERAGEIndian equity, ETF, F&O universe since 2018 tick-level. US universe at minute resolution for the SEC-filed strategy.
INTEGRITYSurvivorship-clean. Point-in-time. Restated fundamentals tagged with as-reported and as-restated versions, both queryable.
iii.
RESEARCH ENVIRONMENT · QX

QX — where ideas become strategies.

~100ideas per year
~2survive to live

QX is the research stack: notebooks, a factor library, a back-test framework, walk-forward and regime-test tooling, paper-trading scaffolding, and the connective tissue that ties them to the data lake on one side and to FIST on the other.

The point of QX is not to enable cleverness — the standard tools allow that fine. The point is to enforce honesty. Back-tests in QX cannot quietly use look-ahead data; the framework prevents it. Walk-forward windows are pre-registered before code runs. Paper-trade results are recorded for every candidate strategy, whether it survives or not. The history of every dead idea is preserved alongside the history of every live one.

QX is what makes the funnel in Strategy R&D possible. Without it, the funnel would be a slogan.

LANGUAGEPython end-to-end. Pandas, NumPy, custom factor and regime libraries. Reproducible Jupyter for write-ups.
BACKTESTEvent-driven simulator with point-in-time data binding, realistic transaction costs and slippage, capacity-aware sizing.
VALIDATIONWalk-forward windows pre-registered. Regime tests across at least 2008, 2013, 2018, 2020, 2022. Paper-trade dwell time before live.
PROVENANCEEvery back-test run is versioned with its data snapshot, code commit, and parameter set. Re-runnable years later, exactly.
iv.
AGENT LAYER · JARVIS

Jarvis — the layer above the layers.

2024built
~40%routine-ops time saved

Jarvis is the newest component. It is the LLM-backed agent layer that sits above FIST, QX, and the data lake — the surface the team uses to operate the firm without drowning in keystrokes. Client onboarding documents, KYC follow-ups, draft research notes, monthly client commentary, compliance log summaries, regulatory filings, internal meeting minutes, response drafts — all the work that is necessary but not differentiating.

The principle is narrow: Jarvis does not make investment decisions. It does not trade. It does not recommend. It writes drafts, summarises, formats, and routes. Every artefact it produces is reviewed by a human before it leaves the firm. The leverage is in the keystrokes saved, not in the judgments outsourced.

This sounds modest. It compounds. The team has roughly forty percent more attention available for the work that actually matters — research, client relationships, risk — than it did before Jarvis existed.

MODELBest-available frontier model via API. Provider-agnostic abstraction so the firm can swap as models improve.
SCOPEDrafting, summarising, formatting, routing. No autonomous trading. No autonomous advice. Every output reviewed by a named person.
DATAFirm-internal context only. Client PII never leaves the firm’s environment; processed under enterprise data-residency terms.
AUDITEvery Jarvis-touched document carries a provenance trail. The reviewing human is named. Logs retained per SEBI record-keeping norms.

a.Weekly operations

The most fragile thing about a quantitative system is not the algorithm. It is the part of the operation that runs at three in the morning on a Saturday, without supervision, and must work. The list below is a representative Saturday at the firm. It is what happens whether or not anyone shows up.

None of it is glamorous. All of it is mandatory. Software that does not run reliably on Saturday at 3am cannot be trusted with client capital on Tuesday at 10am.

vi. OPEN SOURCE
What the firm publishes
from its own stack

The infrastructure, given back.

a.What is — and isn’t — open

The live strategies are not open. They are our edge. The infrastructure around them — data utilities, a backtest kernel, factor toolkits — is gradually being published. Work we did once, so the next person doesn’t have to.

None of these projects is a marketing artefact. They are libraries we use internally, refactored carefully enough to be readable by someone outside the firm. Pull requests are reviewed by the same engineers who use them in production.
