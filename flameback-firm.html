i. ORIGIN
Founded 2017
Bangalore, India

How a small team decided to build the firm differently.

a.Founding

The firm began with two convictions and one frustration.

The first: quantitative methods, applied with discipline over many years, beat discretionary stock-picking after fees, costs, and the steady loss of star managers. This wasn’t new. It was the academic consensus, the AQR-Fama-French insight, and the record across every developed market over fifty years.

The second: nobody offered this approach to private clients on honest terms. The PMS industry was largely discretionary. Mutual funds chased scale. Bank wealth arms sold products. No quantitative private wealth firm took the method seriously, kept its alignment honest, and worked with clients one at a time.

The frustration was personal. Each founder had, separately, been on the receiving end of bad wealth management — as clients, or watching family get sold structured products they didn’t understand by people whose incentives didn’t match theirs. The firm came out of that frustration as much as any business plan.

b.The engineering-first decision

At the founding meeting in a small office in Indiranagar, the team made one decision that proved harder to keep than to make: everyone on the investment side would also code. No split between ‘quants’ who build models and ‘portfolio managers’ who run them. One team. One stack. One discipline.

Nine years later, this is still the firm’s defining choice. The fund manager, the chief technology officer, the research head — each has commits in the production codebase from this month. Strategies aren’t drawn up in PowerPoint and handed to engineers. They are written, tested, and deployed by the same people who understand what they are for.

It also means we hire slowly. Most candidates fail one of two tests — the financial-reasoning side or the engineering side. Of every hundred CVs, fewer than three lead to an interview. Of those, fewer than one in five gets an offer.

ii. WHY WE EXIST
The question
The firm exists to answer

How do you stay invested through hard times.

a.The problem

Markets are uncertain by nature. People are not built to hold positions through drawdowns of 20%, 35%, sometimes 50%. The data is overwhelming and not in dispute: investor returns lag investment returns by 1% to 3% a year across every developed market, product category, and time horizon studied. The gap isn’t fees. It’s behaviour — selling at the wrong time, buying at the wrong time, refusing to sit still in the very periods when sitting still is what compounds.

This is not a failing of investors. It is part of being human. The same stress response that kept our ancestors alive is what makes a smart forty-year-old founder sell a long-term portfolio at the bottom of a drawdown.

The wealth industry has long tried to fix this with appeals to discipline — ‘stay the course’, ‘trust the process’, ‘think long-term’. They don’t work. They haven’t for fifty years. The body’s response does not care about a pep talk.

A strategy you cannot hold through its worst quarter is not, in any meaningful sense, your strategy.
b.The firm’s answer

Our approach is to take these emotional decisions off your plate. The strategies cut exposure automatically when volatility rises. Drawdown control is built into the construction. Position sizes are risk-budgeted, not conviction-weighted. If you panic during a drawdown, you’ll find we’ve already done something measured about it — and that the response was written down, in advance, with the same explanation you got when you signed on.

This is no guarantee against losses. It is a commitment that losses, when they come, are the losses the system was designed to take — not losses caused by an emotional override. The first kind you recover from with patience. The second kind compounds against you.

That is what we mean by ‘all-weather’. Not that the firm never loses money. But that when it does, the loss is part of the strategy, not a break from it.

iv. OFFICE
The space
The discipline

Everything in its place, applied to wealth.

The firm runs on one principle borrowed from the kitchen: everything in its place — ready for use, nothing left undone. We apply it across every part of the operation.

In the kitchen, it means the chef has every ingredient chopped, measured, and arranged before service begins. No scrambling during the work itself. The pressure moves upstream, into the calm hours of preparation. When the orders arrive, all that’s left is execution.

Wealth management works the same way. The calm hours of preparation are when strategies are researched, tested, validated, documented, and stress-checked. The orders arriving are the daily flow of market moves, client questions, filings, and operational tasks. Prepare well, and the daily flow isn’t stressful — it’s just execution.

We pair it with one more habit: always improving. Every live strategy is reviewed monthly. Every process is logged, audited, and shortened. Every quarter, we pick three things that took too long and cut them in half.

And to Seiri and Seiton — sort and set in order. Files are named consistently. Code is reviewed before it merges. Compliance filings are done weeks early. Visitors say the office is quieter than they expect a financial firm to be.

None of this is decoration. It is what lets a team of fifteen manage a hundred crores responsibly — and keep managing it, indefinitely, in good markets and bad.

v. ON GAMES
James Carse
Berkshire as exemplar

Wealth management is an infinite game.

a.Two kinds of games

In 1986, the philosopher James Carse described two kinds of games. Finite games are played to win. They have rules, boundaries, and a clear end. Chess is finite. So is a tennis match. So is a quarterly earnings cycle.

Infinite games are played to keep playing. There is no fixed end. The rules can change. The goal is not to beat the other players but to keep the game going, often by bringing in more of them. Marriage is an infinite game. So is the practice of medicine. So, done well, is managing family wealth across generations.

i. Finite game

Played to win.

Beat the benchmark this quarter. Beat the peer group this year. Raise the next fund. Hit the AUM target. The metrics are clear. The clock is running. The strategy that wins is often the one taking more risk than it looks like it’s taking — because winning the quarter and surviving the decade are different goals, and the finite-game player only needs the first.

Most of the wealth-management industry, by the incentives built into it.

ii. Infinite game

Played to keep playing.

Compound across generations. Stay invested in good markets and bad. Build a practice that outlasts the founders. The metric is continuation, not victory. The strategy that wins is the one that survives the longest run of markets we haven’t seen yet. Berkshire Hathaway has been doing this since 1965. The firm, since 2017.

The firm, by structural choice.

b.Berkshire as the exemplar

We take inspiration from Berkshire Hathaway not for its specific strategies — we don’t pick individual companies, and we have nothing to add on whether See’s Candies should expand — but for how it carries itself. The patience. The refusal to grow faster than it can manage well. The candour in the annual letters. The alignment between the principal’s capital and the shareholders’. The choices that make the firm hard to acquire, hard to disrupt, and easy to compound.

We aim to be, in a quiet and quantitative way, the Berkshire of private wealth management. Not in size. In how we carry ourselves.
