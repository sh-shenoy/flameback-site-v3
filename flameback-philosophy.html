Philosophy.

What the firm believes about markets, about risk, about the human side of compounding wealth — written down so that decisions, when they get hard, can be traced back to first principles rather than to mood.

i. THE FRAME
Why philosophy
matters here

Every investment firm has a philosophy. Most of them are unwritten.

a.Why we wrote it down

A wealth manager makes thousands of small decisions on a client’s behalf each year. Which signals to weight, when to rebalance, what to hold through a drawdown, what to sell, what to leave alone. In most firms, these decisions are made by individuals applying judgement formed by experience, by recent market memory, and by whatever the firm’s culture happens to reward at that moment. The philosophy is the residue of all of that. It exists. It just isn’t written down.

We chose to write ours down for two reasons. The first is that a quantitative firm cannot operate without explicit decision rules. The second is that a relationship-driven firm cannot operate without explicit values. A philosophy document is the place where those two meet. When a strategy is being designed, the philosophy is the constraint. When a client conversation gets difficult, the philosophy is the reference.

What follows is the firm’s position, written in five tenets, four influences, eight books, six observations, and one list of disciplined refusals.

ii. THE FIVE TENETS
What we believe
about markets & capital

Five things the firm believes — and won’t un-believe just because it’s convenient.

i.

Markets are efficient enough to make prediction unreliable, and inefficient enough to reward discipline.

This is the firm’s central wager. We don’t believe in perfect efficiency — the academic literature and the live track records of disciplined quantitative firms over fifty years rule that out. We also don’t believe in exploitable mispricing at the level of individual security selection — the evidence against discretionary stock picking, net of fees, is overwhelming. What we believe is that persistent factor premia exist (momentum, quality, low-volatility, value at the right horizons), that they are noisy, and that they are available to firms with the patience and the engineering to capture them at low cost.

ii.

The constraint on long-term returns is not return. It is behaviour during drawdowns.

If you back-test any reasonable strategy on Indian equities over twenty years, the compounding rate is double-digit. Almost no client experiences double-digit compounding, because almost no client stays invested through the drawdowns. The behavioural gap — the difference between strategy returns and client returns — is consistently measured at three to four percentage points per year across global studies. That gap, compounded, is the difference between merely good wealth and generational wealth. Closing it is the firm’s most important non-quantitative job.

iii.

Time is the active ingredient. Everything else is in service of staying invested through it.

The mathematics of compounding is non-linear. Twenty years at 12% is not twice as much wealth as ten years at 12%; it is more than four times as much. This is the single most powerful fact in finance, and the single most-ignored. The firm’s engagement model, its diversification approach, its drawdown-control architecture, its client-communication discipline — all of it is engineered around one objective: keep the client invested for the time it takes for compounding to do its work.

iv.

Diversification of sources of return matters more than diversification of holdings.

Holding fifty stocks does not diversify a portfolio if all fifty are driven by the same factor exposure to the same regime. Real diversification is across uncorrelated drivers: momentum versus value, quality versus carry, India versus the US, equity versus credit, long versus short. The firm’s strategies are constructed so that each one earns its place in a portfolio by adding a return stream that is genuinely different from the others — not just a different label on the same exposure.

v.

A wealth manager’s most important product is candour.

Most of the wealth-management industry runs on selling clients products they don’t need at fees they don’t see. The firm’s opposite stance is not a marketing position; it is a structural one. We charge transparent fees, we disclose conflicts where they exist, we publish our research and our drawdowns, we say no to product launches that would be lucrative but wrong, and we tell clients things they don’t want to hear when telling them what they want to hear would harm their long-term outcome. Candour is a discipline. It costs the firm money in the short run. It is the only way we know to compound trust in the long run.

iii. CONTRAST
Where the firm parts
from the consensus

Five places we’ve looked at the consensus and quietly walked the other way.

a.Read across

On each row, the left column is what the industry typically tells clients. The right column is what the firm believes — and what the firm’s strategies, fee structure, and conversations actually reflect.

Industry consensus

A good portfolio manager beats the market by picking the right stocks.

The pitch is alpha through skill. The fee is justified by outperformance. The track record is presented through favourable windows.

Our position

A good manager compounds reliably across regimes by harvesting persistent premia at low cost.

The job is not to predict. It is to capture, repeatedly and cheaply, what the academic and empirical literature has identified as durable sources of return.

Industry consensus

Asset allocation should be adjusted to the market view.

Risk on, risk off. Tilt to the asset class with the better setup. Move in and out of equities based on the macro call.

Our position

Asset allocation should reflect client circumstances, not market conviction.

Adjusting allocation to short-term market views is closet market-timing, and it loses on average. Allocation is set against the client’s liabilities, horizon, and behaviour, not the manager’s outlook.

Industry consensus

Diversification means holding more things.

Spread the portfolio across sectors, geographies, asset classes — the wider, the safer.

Our position

Diversification means holding things that fail at different times.

Correlation, not count, is what matters. Twelve equity funds with similar factor exposures are one position with twelve labels.

Industry consensus

The relationship manager’s job is to maintain the client.

Make them feel reassured. Don’t bring up bad news. Stay positive about the markets and the portfolio.

Our position

The relationship manager’s job is to tell the truth about the portfolio.

Clients are owed an honest account, especially during drawdowns. The discomfort of a candid conversation is part of what we’re paid for.

Industry consensus

Growth is the firm’s primary objective. More AUM is always better.

Scale the AUM. Hire more relationship managers. Open more offices. The economics demand it.

Our position

Capacity is the firm’s primary constraint. The right size is the size that lets us do the work properly.

Some of our strategies have capacity limits. Some of our relationships require depth. We turn business away when the alternative would be to do worse work.

iv. INFLUENCES
The intellectual lineage
the firm draws from

Four schools of thought, eight books, one pull-quote — the firm’s reading list.

a.Four schools

The firm’s approach didn’t emerge in isolation. It draws — honestly and deliberately — from four intellectual traditions, each of which has shaped specific elements of how we build strategies and how we work with clients.

School i

The quantitative tradition.

Renaissance, AQR, Two Sigma, Citadel. The conviction that markets are systems, that signals are noisy but persistent, and that disciplined statistical work over long horizons is the most reliable path to alpha. From this tradition we take the empirical method, the suspicion of conviction without evidence, and the idea that a strategy is only as good as its out-of-sample behaviour.

School ii

The value-investing tradition.

Graham, Buffett, Munger. The insistence that ownership of a business at the right price is a real economic activity, not a trade. The discipline of patience, of independence from market sentiment, of saying no to the vast majority of opportunities. We don’t pick stocks the way Buffett does. But the temperament — long horizons, low turnover, contempt for marketing — is the same.

School iii

The risk-and-uncertainty tradition.

Taleb, Mandelbrot, Soros on reflexivity, Kahneman. The acknowledgement that markets are not normal, that tail risk is the risk that matters, that human cognition systematically misjudges probability, and that any system without explicit drawdown control will eventually be destroyed by an event it didn’t plan for. From this tradition comes the firm’s obsession with regime testing, drawdown architecture, and behavioural-finance literacy.

School iv

The infinite-game tradition.

James Carse, Simon Sinek as a populariser, and the long-cycle thinking of Berkshire Hathaway. The framing that wealth management is an infinite game — played not to win but to keep playing — reorganises everything. It changes what counts as a good year, what counts as a good client, what counts as a good fee structure. The firm operates as if it intends to be here in 2046, because it does.

“The investor’s chief problem — and even his worst enemy — is likely to be himself.” Benjamin Graham, The Intelligent Investor, 1949. Quoted in the firm’s first principles document, and in most client conversations during drawdowns.
b.Eight books

The books below are the ones the firm hands to new analysts, references in client letters, and returns to when a hard call has to be made. They’re not a comprehensive list of important investing books. They’re the eight that shaped this firm’s working assumptions.

Read iThe Intelligent InvestorBenjamin Graham, 1949The temperamental foundation. Margin of safety, Mr. Market, and the idea that behaviour is the moat.
Read iiPoor Charlie’s AlmanackCharles T. Munger, 2005On mental models, the latticework of disciplines, and the discipline of inversion. The firm’s favourite chapter is the psychology of human misjudgement.
Read iiiExpected ReturnsAntti Ilmanen, 2011The most rigorous single-volume treatment of factor premia, risk premia, and how to reason about returns across asset classes. Required reading for the research team.
Read ivThe Black SwanNassim Nicholas Taleb, 2007On the failure of normal-distribution thinking and the centrality of tail risk. The firm’s drawdown-control architecture is downstream of this argument.
Read vThinking, Fast and SlowDaniel Kahneman, 2011The behavioural-finance canon in one volume. Loss aversion, anchoring, narrative bias — the cognitive errors that destroy long-term returns.
Read viFinite and Infinite GamesJames P. Carse, 1986The book that gave the firm its operating frame. We’re playing an infinite game. Every strategic choice flows from that.
Read viiThe Man Who Solved the MarketGregory Zuckerman, 2019An account of Renaissance Technologies. Not for the model details — for the culture. The discipline, the suspicion of intuition, the engineering-first instinct.
Read viiiThe Alchemy of FinanceGeorge Soros, 1987On reflexivity — the idea that markets are not just measuring an underlying reality but actively changing it. The deepest available critique of efficient-markets thinking.
v. THE OBSERVATIONS
Where the industry,
quietly, has it wrong

Six things wealth management does that don’t survive scrutiny.

a.Not a critique. An accounting.

This is not a polemic. The industry is staffed by intelligent, generally well-intentioned people working inside structures that don’t reward candour. The list below is the firm’s read of where those structural incentives lead the work in a wrong direction — not a moral judgement of the people inside them.

Performance is reported through favourable windows.
Firms quote the period in which their numbers look best — 3-year if 1-year is poor, since-inception if 5-year is poor, after-tax if pre-tax is poor. Honest reporting is a calendar-fixed comparison to a stated benchmark over a horizon long enough to be meaningful. The firm publishes 1-, 3-, 5-, and since-inception, all of them, on every factsheet.
Fees are described in basis points to make them feel small.
“Just 1.5 per cent” sounds modest. Over twenty years, a 1.5% recurring fee consumes roughly a third of the gross return. The honest framing is the cumulative drag on terminal wealth, which is what the firm shows clients during the engagement-terms conversation.
Asset allocation is presented as personalised when it’s formulaic.
Most “personalised” allocations are one of six standard buckets based on a five-question risk-tolerance quiz. Real personalisation requires understanding the client’s liabilities, business interests, behavioural history, and family structure — not a sliding scale of ‘conservative to aggressive.’
Diversification is sold as protection when it’s frequently just label-spreading.
A client with twelve mutual funds across four AMCs is rarely diversified in any meaningful sense. The underlying factor exposures overlap heavily. Real diversification requires understanding what each holding earns its return from — which most allocators have never been taught to do.
Drawdowns are presented as anomalies when they’re features.
Every honest long-horizon strategy will see drawdowns. The firm tells clients up front that a 20% drawdown is a normal feature, not an emergency. Most firms wait until the drawdown happens and then explain it — which is exactly when the client is least able to hear it.
Marketing is dressed up as research.
Most “market outlook” documents are sales material with a research disclaimer at the bottom. They tell a story consistent with whatever the firm is currently selling. The firm’s position is that genuine research is published with its methodology and its failures alongside its findings — or it isn’t research. See the open-source page for what we publish.
vi. WHAT WE WILL NOT
The disciplined refusals
that define the firm

The line items that show up nowhere at this firm — on purpose.

a.A philosophy is what you refuse

Some of the most important sentences in a firm’s philosophy are the ones that begin with “we will not.” They are harder to enforce than the ones that begin with “we will,” because every refusal eventually meets a moment when it would be lucrative to break. The list below has been broken zero times in nine years. It will not be broken in the years ahead either.

ManifestoThe firm will not.
i.Predict markets in public. We will not write a year-end “outlook,” we will not appear on television calling tops and bottoms, and we will not publish forecasts that the firm’s own strategies don’t depend on. Forecasting is entertainment. It corrodes a quantitative practice.
ii.Sell products we wouldn’t buy ourselves. Every strategy the firm offers has founders’ capital invested alongside client capital. If we wouldn’t put our own money in it, it doesn’t go in the catalogue.
iii.Take commissions from product manufacturers. The firm is fee-only. No revenue from selling third-party insurance products, third-party mutual funds, structured notes, or any other arrangement that creates a conflict between the client’s interest and the firm’s.
iv.Time the market at the portfolio level. Strategies have internal risk controls that may de-gross under specific quantitative conditions. That is not market-timing. Market-timing is a discretionary call by a human looking at headlines. The firm does not do that.
v.Grow beyond capacity. Some strategies have capacity limits. Some relationships require depth. When growth would dilute either, the firm declines the additional business. This has happened. It will happen again.
vi.Hide the drawdowns. All performance reporting includes drawdown statistics. Worst calendar year, worst rolling 12-month period, peak-to-trough, time-to-recovery. The drawdowns are part of the track record. They are not optional.
vii.Run a separate investment policy for the partners than for the clients. The same strategies, the same risk controls, the same fee structure. The partners get no edge from being inside the firm. This is not a marketing claim; it is auditable.
viii.Trade on the firm’s own account against the strategies’ positions. Personal trading by all members of the investment team is pre-cleared, restricted to a published universe, and monitored quarterly. The investment team eats from the same pot as the clients.
