i. THE FRAME
What ‘R&D’ means
at this firm

At a discretionary firm, R&D is a meeting. At a quantitative firm, R&D is a process.

a.Process, not opinion

In a discretionary firm, the question “why do we hold this position?” eventually reaches a person. A portfolio manager, a head of research, a CIO. The answer is some mix of fundamentals, narrative, and conviction. The position exists because someone, somewhere, decided.

At a quantitative firm, the question reaches a process. The position exists because a signal, tested through a defined pipeline, met a set of pre-committed criteria. The signal exists because an idea was specified, back-tested, walk-forward-validated, regime-tested, and paper-traded. Each stage had pre-defined hurdles. The strategy crossed them. That is why the position exists.

The process below is our research pipeline. It is what stands between an idea and your capital. It exists to protect you from our own enthusiasm.

iii. THE STAGES IN DETAIL
What actually happens
at each stage

What it takes for an idea to survive each step.

a.The discipline at each stage

Each of the five stages has its own pre-defined criteria, its own failure modes, and its own gatekeepers. Below, in order, is what each stage actually involves.

i.
Stage one

Hypothesis. The idea is specified in writing.

~100/yrIdeas specified

Before a single line of back-test code is written, the idea goes into a hypothesis memo. The memo answers seven questions: what is the signal? why should it work economically? what is the expected horizon? what regime should kill it? what is the expected return profile? what is its capacity? what is the cleanest test of it?

The research team reviews the memo. Ideas that can’t survive it — because the economic logic is too thin, the expected performance is implausible, or a faster failure mode is already known — are rejected here, before any data is touched. This stage costs almost nothing. Skipping it is one of the most expensive mistakes in quantitative research.

Owns itResearch analyst who specifies the hypothesis
Reviews itWeekly research meeting; two senior reviewers
Kill criteriaThin economic logic, implausible expected return, untestable specification
ii.
Stage two

Back-test. The signal meets clean history.

20 yrsOf clean data

The signal is computed against twenty years of point-in-time-corrected market data. Performance, volatility, drawdown, turnover, and gross/net Sharpe are all measured. Costs are modelled realistically: bid-ask spread, impact, brokerage, taxes. Most ideas die here — the signal that looked promising in concept turns out to be too noisy, too expensive to trade, or too dependent on a single regime.

The back-test report is standardised: the full equity curve, monthly returns, the regime decomposition, the factor attribution, and a side-by-side against a stated benchmark. Any back-test another analyst can’t reproduce from scratch using only the hypothesis memo is rejected on procedural grounds.

Owns itResearch analyst who runs the back-test in the QX environment
Reviews itIndependent reproduction by a second analyst
Kill criteriaNet Sharpe below threshold, drawdown exceeds tolerance, capacity too small, behaviour too regime-dependent
iii.
Stage three

Walk-forward and regime testing.

5+Regimes tested

Walk-forward testing simulates the strategy as it would have run in the past, using only information available at each historical point. Parameter estimation, signal computation, position sizing — all with no look-ahead. If a strategy looks good in a static back-test but degrades in walk-forward, the original result was probably an in-sample artefact.

Regime testing isolates how the strategy behaves in specific historical periods: the 2008 financial crisis, the 2013 taper tantrum, the 2018 small-cap drawdown in India, the COVID shock of 2020, the 2022 rate-driven equity drawdown. A strategy that does well in three of five regimes is not yet a viable candidate. We want strategies that survive regimes, not strategies that exploit them.

Owns itResearch analyst in collaboration with risk team
Reviews itCTO and fund manager review walk-forward results jointly
Kill criteriaWalk-forward Sharpe falls below 60% of static, fails any one defined regime, behaves discontinuously across regimes
iv.
Stage four

Paper trading. Live data, simulated execution.

6+ moMinimum runway

The strategy now runs on live market data through the FIST trade engine, generating positions and tracking returns — but no real orders go to the market. This stage tests what history cannot: signal latency, real-time data quality, execution slippage against realistic limit-order behaviour, and the discipline of running the strategy day after day.

Six months is our minimum paper-trading runway, twelve preferred. The longer window catches strategies whose live behaviour drifts from the back-test in ways that take time to surface — capacity limits in thinly traded names, microstructure issues at the open, signal degradation that only shows up in specific conditions.

Owns itFIST engineering team in collaboration with the originating analyst
Reviews itMonthly review of paper-traded performance against back-test forecast
Kill criteriaLive performance diverges from back-test, slippage exceeds assumption, capacity reveals as lower than modelled, operational fragility
v.
Stage five

Live capital. Founders’ money goes in first.

6–12 moFounders’ only

The strategy moves to live capital — but the first capital is the firm’s own. Founders’ capital, partners’ capital, and the firm’s strategic balance sheet. For six to twelve months, it runs only on internal money. Performance is tracked against the paper-trading forecast. Discipline is observed under real P&L.

Only after this period — and only if live performance matches the paper-traded forecast — does the strategy reach client portfolios. Even then, it starts small. It earns its weight by its live track record, not its back-test. This is the longest, most expensive stage of the funnel. It is also the only one that actually verifies anything.

Owns itFund manager in coordination with the firm’s investment committee
Reviews itMonthly investment committee, quarterly partner review
Kill criteriaLive performance diverges from paper-trade by more than tolerance, drawdown exceeds projection, operational issues recur
iv. FAILURE MODES
The recurring ways
strategies fail in the pipeline

Six things that kill strategies. All six are familiar.

a.The known failure modes

After nine years of running this pipeline, the failure modes repeat. Almost every strategy that dies, dies for one of the six reasons below. Spotting them earlier is part of what has made our R&D faster over time, without making it any less disciplined.

i.

Overfitting.

The strategy looks brilliant in-sample because its parameters were optimised against the same data used to test it. In walk-forward, the performance collapses. The cardinal sin of quantitative research. It is also the most preventable.

ii.

Transaction costs were under-modelled.

The back-test assumed clean execution at mid-prices. Real-world bid-ask spread, market impact, and brokerage eat most of the gross return. This is the most common reason a promising back-test fails at paper-trading.

iii.

Capacity is smaller than modelled.

The strategy works on a small book but cannot be scaled without moving the prices it depends on. Useful at ₹5 crore; useless at ₹50 crore. Capacity analysis happens at stage two; many ideas die there for this reason alone.

iv.

Regime dependence.

The strategy works beautifully in one type of market and breaks in another. A strategy that needs trending markets fails in mean-reverting ones. The firm prefers strategies that are slightly worse on average but consistent across regimes.

v.

Data quality issues.

The back-test result depended on a feature that wasn’t cleanly available in the data — survivorship bias, look-ahead bias, restated fundamentals, point-in-time inaccuracy. Often invisible until the back-test is reproduced from a different data source.

vi.

Signal decay.

The signal worked in the past but has been arbitraged away. Or it depends on a market structure that no longer exists. This is why live tracking matters — some signals quietly stop working, and only careful monitoring catches them.

v. THE CALENDAR
How research time
is allocated

Research time, budgeted.

a.Time is the scarce resource

Research time is the most valuable resource we have. It is also the easiest to waste. Without an explicit budget, it drifts toward whatever is most interesting or most recent. The split below is how we allocate it deliberately.

40%Stage one & two: new ideas.Specifying hypotheses and running first-pass back-tests. The highest-volume, lowest-yield part of the funnel.
25%Stage three & four: validation.Walk-forward, regime testing, and paper-trading supervision. The most patient, most disciplined part of the work.
20%Live monitoring of in-production strategies.Performance attribution, regime checks, signal-decay monitoring, capacity reviews. A live strategy is never finished.
15%Infrastructure & data work.Data lake improvements, FIST upgrades, research-tool maintenance. The substrate everything else runs on.
vi. OPEN R&D
What the firm publishes
from the research pipeline

Some of the work doesn’t become a strategy. It becomes a published note.

a.Publishing the work

Plenty of research gets done and validated but never becomes a live strategy — the capacity is too small, the signal too well-known, or the practical edge too thin. Where the work itself is interesting, we publish it. The three destinations below are open to the public.

Open i

QLab Notes.

Working-paper-style write-ups of research the firm has done. Methodology, results, and failures. The discipline of writing it down is part of the discipline of doing it.

Read → Open ii

Alpha Feed.

Shorter posts — observations from the research pipeline, market microstructure notes, occasional commentary. Faster cadence, less formal than QLab Notes.

Read → Open iii

Open source.

The firm publishes selected research code — data utilities, analysis libraries, factor toolkits. Not the live strategies. The infrastructure around them.
